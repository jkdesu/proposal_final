01

Research Question

"How can real-time visual tools be designed to measure and communicate price differences in daily consumer goods between cities, and how do individuals emotionally respond to these differences when presented dynamically?"

Through a comparison between Tokyo and New York City, this project investigates how people react to the price changes of the same item. I think when real-time visual tools reveal price differences between Tokyo and New York City, individuals will display stronger emotional reactions to goods with higher perceived price elasticity—everyday essentials whose prices are expected to remain stable. This suggests that emotional response patterns can function as a behavioral measure of elasticity, revealing how sensitive people feel to unexpected price changes.

02

Project Description

This project combines real-time price visualization with facial-expression analytics to investigate how people emotionally respond to price differences between Tokyo and New York.

01

Economic Volatility

The changing cost of identical items across cities, shown through interactive simulations and cost-of-living calculators.

02

Human Volatility

The rate of change in facial expression when users encounter unexpected price differences, measured through real-time face-tracking.

03-A

Computational Method

My project uses browser-based computer vision, dynamic data rendering, and interactive simulation to analyze how users emotionally respond to cross-city price differences. Real-time facial-expression tracking quantifies volatility as a rate of change in users' reactions, turning emotional responses into measurable data.

Simultaneously, a scroll-driven price engine and cumulative budget calculator visualize how daily expenses accumulate in Tokyo and New York. These computational methods enable an experiential analysis: they reveal not only the numerical gaps between cities but also how people physically and intuitively process those gaps.

The workflow transforms static economic data into an interactive, human-centered interpretation of affordability.

03-B

Design Method

My design methods are primarily personal, experimental, analytic, and narrative, using interaction to expose how economic information shapes emotional experience. By pairing real-time price visualization with facial-expression tracking, the project creates a participatory environment.

By centering my project around cities I live in, this project becomes personal.

03-C

Precedent

Big Mac Index

The Economist's Big Mac Index is a key precedent for my project because it uses a single, everyday commodity to reveal global price disparities through a playful yet rigorous economic lens.

Its PPP calculator demonstrates how a familiar good can serve as a microeconomic indicator of affordability across cities. My project extends this idea by shifting from a static comparison tool to an interactive, real-time emotional and computational experience.

My Final Project Plan

I had a chance to develop an interactive project that lets users have a spatial experience during Spatial UX class at GSAPP. (group project)

For my final project, I aim to create a spatial experience where people can scroll through items spatially where all the images are portrayed on the wall. In that case, I could make a more personal and spatial experience.

I want to have separate rooms for Tokyo and NYC so that people actually feel like they are experiencing being in two different cities.

I also will implement more variety of options for living expenses as well as jobs.

I want this project to expand more spatially because I think having a physical experience will have more impact on the rate of change in facial expression rather than looking at items on the screen.
